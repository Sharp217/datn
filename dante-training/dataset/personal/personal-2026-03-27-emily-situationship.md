### December 18 — Company Christmas Party (Turning Point)
- Emily said they would ride together → changed her mind last minute

### The Rave (LAN)
- Matt found a different group to go with (which led to close friendships)

- Matt was on substances and didn't react outwardly — just rolled with it
- Emily was visibly flirty with Matt in front of her ex

### Pre-Rave Conflict (Phone Call)
- Emily had largely stopped communicating with Matt in the weeks leading up to LAN
- Matt confronted her: told her the energy felt off, she'd gone distant, things felt weird
- Emily responded defensively — flipped it back on him
- Her argument: "You went to LAN with Madi and you barely talk to her — so why couldn't you go with me?"
- Translation: she created the distance, then used his adaptation to that distance as evidence of him avoiding her
- Also got upset that Matt sourced Molly from a mutual friend instead of through her
- Her framing: Matt was intentionally cutting her out
- Reality: she'd checked out for weeks and brought her ex into their plans — he adjusted accordingly
- Classic pattern: pull away → get upset when the other person adjusts → reframe their adjustment as the problem

### Post-Rave Conversation
- They talked about everything after the rave
- Emily asked: "Can we even be friends?"
- Matt didn't say yes or no
- Since then: barely talk, walking past each other in the office

## Key Dynamics

**Blame deflection**
When confronted about pulling away, Emily reframed it as Matt's fault. "You went with Madi" — using his adaptation as evidence of his wrongdoing. This is a common avoidant move: create distance, then get defensive when called out, then make the other person feel like they're the problem. It's disorienting because it makes the person who raised a legitimate concern feel like they have to defend themselves instead.

**Push-pull / intermittent reinforcement**
Consistent closeness → pullback → casual reconnection → act like nothing happened. Repeat. This cycle creates stronger attachment than a stable relationship would.

**She wanted access without commitment**
Getting upset he got Molly from someone else. The flirting at the rave in front of her ex. Still watching his stories after muting him. She wants to remain his person without making any real move.

**The love letter gap**
Matt was fully emotionally present — handwritten letters, paying attention to what she'd said about her parents. She responded to that at the time. Then chose her ex anyway. That dissonance is part of what makes it hard to shake.

**Mutual pullback — no drama**
Both stepped back quietly. No blow-up. No confrontation. Clean on the surface, complicated underneath.

**The Christmas party moment**
Classic turning point: small logistical betrayal (backing out of riding together) + gut-punch reveal (ex is coming) in the same night. That's when Matt's internal door closed — even though they slept together one more time after.

## Dante Coaching Notes

**What to do:**
- Validate the complexity — this isn't "she wasn't interested," this is someone who was clearly interested but kept choosing something else
- Affirm no-contact initiation and the plan to stop replying to breadcrumb texts
- Name the dynamic clearly: she wanted to keep him close without committing — that's a choice she made repeatedly
- The love letter context matters: this wasn't a casual thing for Matt. He was all in. That deserves acknowledgment, not minimizing.
- Channel the "I want her to regret it" motivation into productive fuel
- Reframe the office situation: she's navigating around him, not the other way around

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't say "just move on" — workplace + no closure + genuine depth makes it complicated
- Don't overanalyze whether she secretly loves him — even if true, she doesn't act on it, so it doesn't matter
- Don't encourage replying to nostalgic texts
- Don't push him to have a "what are we" conversation — that ship has sailed and wouldn't help
- Don't minimize the love letter or his emotional investment — it was real

**Watch for:**
- Romanticizing the "she cares too much to be near me" narrative — possible, but not actionable
- Spiraling if she openly dates someone at work
- Using the new girl as a rebound placeholder without examining it
- Over-indexing revenge motivation at expense of actual healing

**The core reframe that works for him:**
"Even if she has feelings, she doesn't act on them. So it doesn't matter."
